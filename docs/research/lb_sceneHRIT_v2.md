### Scene 13 · Elle's Harvard Admissions Video Essay

**Elle's video essay would not have been considered**

> Elle submits a poolside video essay for Harvard Law, complete with a bikini and a Greek chorus. In reality, admissions committees judge your writing through a personal statement — the video would never have left the mailroom. A Fashion Merchandising 4.0 and a 179 LSAT are what actually got her admitted.

- **Q:** So what does Harvard admissions actually look at?
  **A:** A personal statement, undergraduate transcript, LSAT or GRE score, letters of recommendation, and resume. Writing samples carry more weight than anything visual. The Class of 2028 had a median LSAT of 174 and a median GPA of 3.96.
- **Q:** Was there supposed to be a celebrity cameo in this scene?
  **A:** Yes. The original script had Elle and her sorority sisters chase Judge Judy through the video essay. Judge Judy Sheindlin declined to appear — the cameo was cut entirely.

### Scene 16 · Elle Gets Her LSAT Score

**An LSAT score of 179 is nearly impossible**

> Elle jumps her practice score from a 143 to an official 179. While not legally impossible, a 179 places a test-taker in the 99.9th percentile — a perfect score is 180. Doing this in a matter of months with zero prior legal inclination is the equivalent of a casual jogger suddenly running a 4-minute mile.

- **Q:** What LSAT score does Harvard Law actually want?
  **A:** The median LSAT for an admitted Harvard Law 1L is 174. Elle's 179 didn't just scrape her in — it put her in rarer company than nearly every other applicant that year.
- **Q:** Is it realistic she took the LSAT in a halter dress?
  **A:** Yes. LSAC recommends comfortable clothing but has no formal dress code. If Elle felt most confident in pink, she was entirely within the rules.

### Scene 17 · The Harvard Admissions Committee

**Admissions wouldn't vote someone in just for "diversity"**

> The committee admits Elle because they need variety and her video essay is amusing. In reality, a candidate with a 4.0 GPA and a 179 LSAT is a near-automatic "yes" at any law school purely on the numbers — no bikini video required. The committee debate was dramatized; the math admitted her.

- **Q:** Does a Fashion Merchandising degree hurt your chances?
  **A:** Not at all. Law schools love academic variety. A 4.0 in Fashion Merchandising legitimately stands out alongside the typical Political Science and History majors.
- **Q:** Would Elle's sorority leadership count?
  **A:** Yes. Being Delta Nu's president and orchestrating a charity drive — even one involving faux fur panties — is real leadership. Admissions committees weigh extracurriculars heavily.

### Scene 18 · Elle Arrives at Harvard

**This isn't actually Harvard**

> None of Elle's Harvard scenes were filmed at Harvard. The university refused the production, as did Stanford and USC — a USC official told the producers, "we felt there was too much stereotyping going on." Every Cambridge shot you see is California stand-ins.

- **Q:** So where were the Harvard scenes filmed?
  **A:** UCLA, Caltech, and Rose City High School in Pasadena stood in for the campus. Only a few aerial shots of the real Cambridge appear in the final cut.
- **Q:** Where was the graduation scene shot?
  **A:** Dulwich College in London. Reese was already there filming The Importance of Being Earnest, so the crew flew to her. Both she and Luke Wilson wore wigs — both had cut their hair for follow-up films.

### Scene 22 · Elle Gets Kicked Out of Stromwell's Class

**You don't get kicked out for failing a cold-call**

> Professor Stromwell ejects Elle for not knowing the reading. The Socratic method is real — professors cold-call and layer increasingly hard questions — but you stay in your seat. Per attorney Michael J. Helfand in the ABA Student Lawyer: "You'd get embarrassed by the professor, but you wouldn't be asked to leave."

- **Q:** Who is Professor Stromwell based on?
  **A:** The harsh-law-professor archetype traces to Edward Henry "Bull" Warren (1873–1945), a real Harvard Law professor famous for his "Spartan training" and classroom rudeness. He also inspired Professor Kingsfield in The Paper Chase.
- **Q:** Does "look to your left, one of you won't be here" hold up?
  **A:** Harvard Law itself calls that speech an urban legend. The school has shifted to a deliberately collaborative culture — and with a 9.2% admit rate, most students who get in do finish.

### Scene 37 · Callahan's Criminal Law Class

**Elle's definition of mens rea is 100% accurate**

> Elle correctly answers that diminished capacity is used to negate *mens rea*. This is real criminal law doctrine. *Mens rea* means "guilty mind," and proving a defendant lacked the mental capacity to form intent is a legitimate — if difficult — defense strategy.

- **Q:** What does *actus reus* mean?
  **A:** *Mens rea* is the guilty mind; *actus reus* is the guilty act. To convict someone of most crimes, the prosecution has to prove both — that the person did the act, and that they had the mental intent to do it.
- **Q:** Was this a real Socratic-method moment?
  **A:** Mildly. Callahan posed a question to the room rather than cold-calling, which let Elle volunteer. Defining a concept in your own words rather than reciting the textbook is a hallmark of a good law student.

### Scene 38 · Paulette's Custody "Case"

**Habeas corpus is for prisoners, not pets**

> Elle threatens Paulette's ex with "habeas corpus" to get the dog back. It sounds intimidating but is legally incorrect. *Habeas corpus* is a writ to bring a detained human before a court. The correct action for recovering wrongfully withheld property — including a dog — is a writ of replevin.

- **Q:** Are pets considered property?
  **A:** Yes. Despite how we feel about them, the legal system categorizes pets as personal property — similar to a car or a piece of furniture.
- **Q:** Did Elle commit a crime by taking Rufus?
  **A:** Technically, taking the dog without a court order could be trespass or theft. But because Dewey handed the dog over voluntarily under Elle's bluff, they bypassed the legal system entirely.

### Scene 44 · Callahan Brings Elle onto the Brooke Windham Case

**1Ls don't work on high-profile murder cases**

> Callahan puts Elle, Warner, and Vivian on his firm's active murder defense team. In reality, first-year law students have zero courtroom experience and haven't taken Criminal Procedure. A high-powered partner would never risk his license or his client's life by putting 1Ls on the front lines.

- **Q:** What do 1L interns actually do?
  **A:** Library work. Legal research, drafting internal memos, checking citations, and summarizing case law for the real attorneys. Per ABA attorney Michael J. Helfand, it would also be highly unusual for a professor to bring students into their outside firm at all.
- **Q:** Where do 1Ls get real legal experience, then?
  **A:** School-run clinics. The Harvard Legal Aid Bureau — founded in 1913, the oldest student-run legal services office in the US — trained Michelle Obama, Loretta Lynch, and Justice William Brennan under supervised practice.

### Scene 50 · Elle Learns About Brooke's Alibi

**Elle misunderstands attorney-client privilege**

> Elle refuses to tell Callahan about Brooke's liposuction alibi, citing the "bonds of sisterhood." As an intern, Elle is an agent of the firm — her duty of confidentiality is to the client, not to her friend group. Withholding case-critical information from lead counsel is an ethics violation, not loyalty.

- **Q:** Could Callahan have fired her for this?
  **A:** Immediately. Withholding an alibi during a capital murder trial is a massive breach of duty. The "sisterhood" defense wouldn't protect her from being fired or reprimanded by the firm.
- **Q:** Did Brooke have the right to keep the alibi a secret?
  **A:** Yes. A client directs the objectives of their defense. If Brooke explicitly told her lawyers not to use the alibi because it would ruin her fitness empire, the lawyers have to abide — even if it means losing the case.

### Scene 61 · Emmett Cross-Examines Enrique

**Trial by ambush doesn't happen in real courts**

> Emmett tricks Enrique into revealing he's gay, destroying his credibility as Brooke's alleged lover. In real courtrooms, "trial by ambush" is forbidden. Both sides exchange witness lists and evidence during discovery, long before the trial begins — there are no surprise reveals.

- **Q:** Would the defense have known about Enrique's boyfriend?
  **A:** Almost certainly. A competent defense team would have hired private investigators to dig into Enrique's background months in advance, turning up his relationship with Chuck before he ever took the stand.
- **Q:** Was Emmett's questioning technique allowed?
  **A:** Yes. The rapid-fire leading-question style designed to slip up a witness is a classic cross-examination move. Leading questions are specifically permitted on cross.

### Scene 67 · Elle Takes Over as Lead Counsel

**Rule 3:03 is real — but Elle couldn't use it**

> Elle dismisses Callahan and takes over as lead counsel, citing Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court Rule 3:03. The rule is real and does allow law students to represent clients. But the student must be a 3L under supervision, limited to civil cases or minor misdemeanors. A 1L cannot lead a capital murder defense.

- **Q:** Why did the judge allow it?
  **A:** Pure Hollywood magic. In reality, the judge would have denied the motion on the spot, noted Elle's lack of qualifications, and granted Brooke a continuance to find a new, licensed attorney.
- **Q:** What's the role of the supervising attorney under 3:03?
  **A:** The supervisor — Emmett, in Elle's case — has to be physically present in the courtroom and takes full professional and ethical responsibility for the student's actions. If Elle had blown the defense, Emmett's law license would have been on the line.

### Scene 68 · Elle Cross-Examines Chutney

**The perm science is actually correct**

> Elle wins the case by arguing wet hair deactivates a fresh perm. The chemistry is real. Ammonium thioglycolate — invented in 1938 by Arnold F. Willatt — breaks the disulfide bonds in hair so it can be reshaped, and water within ~24 hours stops the new bonds from setting. Elle was right.

- **Q:** Would the cross-examination actually work?
  **A:** The chemistry holds. The mid-stand confession doesn't. Per Michael J. Helfand (ABA Student Lawyer): "it's not common for someone to confess to murder on the witness stand." In a real trial you'd expose inconsistencies, not get a full admission.
- **Q:** Does surprise evidence really appear mid-trial like this?
  **A:** Never. Both sides exchange evidence and witness lists in discovery, and courts rule on admissibility in advance. Reviewer Jeannie Kang (Medium, 2023) notes that "mindblowing and unexpected evidence" doesn't appear mid-trial in real practice.
